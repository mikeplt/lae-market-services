Macro Assessment

Today's April NFP release is the defining data point of the week. +115k new jobs (preliminary) came in below the trailing 12-month average – but the context matters: March was revised sharply upward to +185k, bringing the 3-month average to roughly +132k. That is a labor market slowing toward equilibrium, not one breaking down.

The unemployment rate held at 4.3% for the second consecutive month – unchanged from March, and still within a narrow band that has persisted since mid-2025. A headline print that softens while unemployment stays flat is precisely the soft-landing signal the Fed has been engineering. No alarm bells, no acceleration of rate-cut urgency either.

Inflation is making quiet but steady progress. Headline CPI (March 2026) stands at +3.3% YoY, with Core CPI at +2.6% YoY – both down meaningfully from their peaks and converging toward the Fed's 2% target. The narrowing gap between headline and core suggests that energy and food distortions are fading rather than amplifying. The disinflation trend is intact.

For the Fed, today's data is not a catalyst for action – it is confirmation that the current policy stance is working. A rate cut remains on the table for H2 2026, but the Fed needs to see further progress on core inflation before moving. Markets should interpret this as Goldilocks: cool enough to keep easing expectations alive, warm enough to avoid recession fears.

The macro score of 61/100 (BULLISH) reflects an economy that is decelerating in a healthy way. Risk assets remain broadly supported. The primary watch items going forward are May CPI (due mid-June) and whether the NFP trend stabilizes above 100k or continues to drift lower.
